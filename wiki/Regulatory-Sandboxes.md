# AI Regulatory Sandboxes (Article 57)

Article 57 of the EU AI Act requires each Member State to establish at least one **AI regulatory sandbox** by **2 August 2026** — this deadline is **retained under the Digital Omnibus proposal** (unlike the high-risk and Article 50 deadlines, which are pushed to 2027). Regulatory sandboxes provide controlled environments where AI systems can be developed, tested, and validated under regulatory supervision before being placed on the market.

## What Is a Regulatory Sandbox

An AI regulatory sandbox is a **supervised testing environment** established and operated by a national competent authority (typically the market surveillance authority) that allows:

- **Providers** (and prospective providers) of AI systems to develop, train, validate, and test their systems under real-world conditions
- **Deployers** to test AI systems in a controlled setting before full deployment
- Regulatory authorities to gain practical understanding of AI systems and their risks

The sandbox is **not** a regulatory exemption. All participants remain subject to applicable law — including GDPR, product safety rules, and the AI Act — but benefit from regulatory guidance, structured interaction with authorities, and certain protections.

## Key Features (Article 57–62)

### Establishment and Operation

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Deadline** | Each Member State must establish at least one sandbox by 2 August 2026 |
| **Alternatives** | A Member State may participate in a jointly established sandbox with other Member States instead of creating its own, provided the national benefit is equivalent |
| **Supervision** | Sandboxes are supervised by national competent authorities designated under the AI Act |
| **Duration** | Time-limited participation — each sandbox engagement has a defined start and end |
| **Scope** | Scope-limited — each engagement focuses on a specific AI system, use case, and testing plan |

### Legal Protections for Participants

Sandbox participants benefit from specific protections under Article 57:

1. **Regulatory guidance** — direct access to expert advice from the competent authority on compliance requirements
2. **Administrative fine protection** — participants who follow the sandbox plan and act in good faith are protected from administrative fines for certain regulatory infractions occurring within the sandbox
3. **Liability remains** — the protection from fines does **not** exempt participants from civil liability for damages caused to third parties
4. **Exit report** — at the conclusion of sandbox participation, the authority issues a report documenting the activities conducted and their outcomes, which can be used positively in conformity assessments

### Data Processing in Sandboxes (Article 59)

Personal data collected outside the sandbox may be processed within the sandbox for developing, testing, and training AI systems, subject to strict conditions:

- Processing must be necessary for the sandbox purpose
- Standard GDPR compliance remains mandatory (lawful basis, data minimisation, security measures)
- Data must be processed in a functionally separate, isolated, and protected environment
- Data processed in the sandbox must not be transmitted or accessed outside the sandbox
- Personal data must be deleted when sandbox participation ends (unless retention is required by law)
- Logs of data processing must be maintained

### SME and Startup Provisions (Article 62)

The AI Act prioritises sandbox access for small and medium-sized enterprises:

- Member States must ensure that sandbox conditions take into account the interests of SMEs and startups
- Sandbox participation must be accessible regardless of the size of the applying entity
- Fees (if any) must be proportionate and not create barriers for smaller organisations
- The EU AI Office may provide technical support and guidance to sandboxes

## Participation Process

### Typical Steps

1. **Application** — the provider or deployer submits a sandbox application to the national competent authority, including:
   - Description of the AI system
   - Intended purpose and use case
   - Proposed testing plan (scope, duration, data requirements)
   - Risk assessment and mitigation measures
   - Exit criteria

2. **Admission** — the authority evaluates the application and decides on admission. Criteria typically include innovation potential, compliance readiness, and risk management adequacy.

3. **Sandbox agreement** — a written agreement defines the scope, conditions, safeguards, monitoring arrangements, and exit criteria.

4. **Supervised testing** — the participant develops, tests, and validates the AI system under the authority's supervision, with regular reporting and interaction.

5. **Exit and reporting** — at the conclusion:
   - The participant produces an exit report documenting activities, findings, and compliance measures
   - The authority issues its own assessment
   - Sandbox outcomes can be referenced in subsequent conformity assessments

### EU Implementing Regulation

The European Commission published a **draft Implementing Regulation on AI Regulatory Sandboxes** for public consultation in December 2025. This regulation will establish:

- Common rules and procedures for national sandboxes
- Entry criteria and eligibility requirements
- Operational standards and safeguards
- Output documentation requirements
- Cross-border cooperation mechanisms

## Member State Sandbox Status

As of early 2026, sandbox implementation varies across the EU:

| Member State | Status | Lead Authority |
|---|---|---|
| **Spain** | Operational — AESIA sandbox launched in 2024 | Agencia Española de Supervisión de Inteligencia Artificial (AESIA) |
| **France** | Pilot sandbox announced; framework under development | CNIL / sector-specific regulators |
| **Netherlands** | Sandbox programme announced; intake planned for 2026 | ACM / sector-specific regulators |
| **Denmark** | Early sandbox framework established | Danish Business Authority |
| **Germany** | Planning phase; multiple sector-specific sandboxes under discussion | BNetzA / sector-specific authorities |
| **Italy** | Legislative framework under development | AgID / GPDP |
| **Most other Member States** | Planning or early consultation stages | Various |

## Benefits of Sandbox Participation

| Benefit | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Regulatory certainty** | Direct guidance from the competent authority on how the AI Act applies to your specific system |
| **Faster time to market** | Structured compliance pathway with regulatory support reduces uncertainty and delays |
| **Evidence for conformity assessment** | Sandbox exit reports provide documented evidence for subsequent conformity assessments |
| **Competitive advantage** | Early regulatory engagement signals compliance maturity to customers and partners |
| **Fine protection** | Good-faith participation protects against administrative fines within the sandbox scope |
| **Innovation support** | Opportunity to test novel AI approaches that might otherwise be delayed by regulatory uncertainty |

## Implications for Organisations

1. **Monitor sandbox availability** — track which Member States have established sandboxes and how to apply.
2. **Identify candidate AI systems** — novel or complex AI systems that could benefit from regulatory guidance and supervised testing.
3. **Prepare applications early** — sandbox places may be limited; early preparation demonstrates readiness.
4. **Budget for participation** — sandbox engagement requires dedicated resources for testing, documentation, and regulatory interaction.
5. **Cross-border coordination** — if your AI system will be deployed across multiple Member States, consider applying to a sandbox in the primary market.
