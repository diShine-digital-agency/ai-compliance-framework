# Harmonised Standards and the CEN/CENELEC Standardisation Process

Harmonised European standards are a central mechanism of the EU AI Act's compliance framework. Article 40 establishes that high-risk AI systems conforming to harmonised standards are **presumed to comply** with the corresponding requirements of the AI Act. This "presumption of conformity" is the most efficient path to demonstrating compliance.

## What Are Harmonised Standards

Harmonised standards are **European standards** developed by the European Standardisation Organisations (CEN and CENELEC) at the **request of the European Commission** (known as a "standardisation request" or "mandate"). Once adopted and referenced in the Official Journal of the EU, they create a legal presumption of conformity with the corresponding regulatory requirements.

| Concept | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Harmonised standard** | A European standard (EN) developed by CEN/CENELEC to support compliance with a specific EU regulation. Referenced in the Official Journal. |
| **Presumption of conformity** | If you conform to the harmonised standard, you are legally presumed to comply with the corresponding requirements of the regulation (Article 40). The burden shifts to the authority to prove otherwise. |
| **Voluntary** | Conformity with harmonised standards is voluntary — organisations can demonstrate compliance by other means. But standards provide the most straightforward path. |
| **Not ISO** | International standards (ISO/IEC) are not the same as harmonised European standards, although harmonised standards often build upon or reference ISO standards. |

## CEN/CENELEC JTC 21

The European Commission issued a standardisation request to CEN and CENELEC in May 2023, tasking them with developing harmonised standards to support the AI Act. The work is led by **JTC 21** — the Joint Technical Committee on Artificial Intelligence.

### Priority Standardisation Areas

JTC 21 is working on ten priority areas that map to the AI Act's core requirements:

| Priority Area | AI Act Alignment | JTC 21 Workstream |
|---|---|---|
| **Risk management** | Article 9 | Risk management system for AI |
| **Data governance and quality** | Article 10 | Data quality, preparation, annotation, bias assessment |
| **Record keeping and logging** | Article 12 | Logging, traceability, audit trail |
| **Transparency and information provision** | Article 13 | Transparency obligations, user information |
| **Human oversight** | Article 14 | Human oversight mechanisms and design |
| **Accuracy and robustness** | Article 15 | Accuracy metrics, robustness testing, resilience |
| **Cybersecurity** | Article 15 | Cybersecurity requirements for AI systems |
| **Quality management** | Article 17 | Quality management system for AI providers |
| **Conformity assessment** | Articles 43–49 | Procedures for demonstrating compliance |
| **Post-market monitoring** | Article 72 | Systematic monitoring after market placement |

### Standards in Development

Key standards under development include:

| Standard | Topic | Status (Spring 2026) |
|---|---|---|
| **prEN 18286** | AI quality management | Public enquiry completed (October 2025) |
| Standards for risk management | AI-specific risk management | Drafting |
| Standards for data governance | Training data quality and bias | Drafting |
| Standards for transparency | Information provision and explainability | Drafting |
| Standards for testing and validation | Accuracy, robustness, adversarial testing | Drafting |

**Note:** As of spring 2026, most AI Act-specific harmonised standards remain in development. None have yet been officially cited in the Official Journal of the EU, which means the presumption of conformity is **not yet available** for most AI Act requirements.

## The Compliance Gap

The high-risk AI system obligations become enforceable on **2 December 2027** under the Digital Omnibus proposal (**2 August 2026** pre-Omnibus), but harmonised standards are not expected to be fully published and referenced until 2027. This creates a **compliance gap**:

| Date | Situation |
|---|---|
| **2 August 2026** *(pre-Omnibus binding date)* | High-risk obligations enforceable if Omnibus not adopted; harmonised standards not yet published |
| **2027** | Standards expected to be finalised and published in the Official Journal |
| **2 December 2027** *(Omnibus)* | High-risk obligations fully enforceable under Omnibus timeline |
| **After OJ publication** | Presumption of conformity available |

### What to Do During the Gap

Organisations cannot wait for harmonised standards before preparing for compliance. During the gap:

1. **Use existing international standards as guidance** — ISO/IEC 42001, ISO/IEC 23894, and ISO/IEC 27001 provide relevant frameworks, even though they do not create a presumption of conformity under the AI Act.
2. **Follow Commission guidelines** — the European Commission has published and will continue to publish guidance documents that interpret AI Act requirements.
3. **Document your approach** — maintain detailed records of how you have addressed each AI Act requirement, what standards and guidance you have followed, and what evidence supports your compliance claim.
4. **Monitor JTC 21 progress** — track the development of harmonised standards and prepare to adopt them as they become available.
5. **Engage with the standardisation process** — organisations can participate in JTC 21 through their national standardisation body (e.g., UNI in Italy, DIN in Germany, BSI in the UK, AFNOR in France).

## The Six-Step Development Process

Harmonised standards follow a formal development process:

| Step | Activity | Status for AI Act Standards |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Standardisation request** from the Commission to CEN/CENELEC | ✅ Completed (May 2023) |
| 2 | **Drafting** by JTC 21 working groups | 🔄 In progress |
| 3 | **Public enquiry** — open comment period | 🔄 Started for some standards |
| 4 | **ESO voting** — formal vote by national standardisation bodies | ⏳ Pending |
| 5 | **Publication** — adopted as European Standards (EN) | ⏳ Pending |
| 6 | **Reference in the Official Journal** — Commission assessment and citation | ⏳ Pending |

Only after step 6 does the standard create a legal presumption of conformity.

## Relationship to ISO Standards

| ISO Standard | Relationship to Harmonised Standards |
|---|---|
| **ISO/IEC 42001:2023** (AIMS) | Foundation for AI governance; harmonised standards are expected to reference its principles, but ISO 42001 certification alone does not create a presumption of conformity |
| **ISO/IEC 23894:2023** (AI risk management) | Relevant guidance for the risk management harmonised standard |
| **ISO/IEC 27001:2022** (Information security) | Referenced for cybersecurity aspects of AI systems |
| **ISO/IEC 25059:2023** (AI quality model) | Input to the quality management harmonised standard |

See [ISO 42001 and AI Governance Standards](ISO-42001-and-AI-Governance-Standards) for detail on ISO 42001's practical value despite not creating a presumption of conformity.

## Common Specifications (Fallback Mechanism)

If harmonised standards are not adopted or are insufficient, the European Commission can adopt **common specifications** (Article 41) as a fallback. Common specifications are technical specifications adopted by the Commission that providers can follow to demonstrate compliance. Conformity with common specifications creates a presumption of conformity, similar to harmonised standards.

This fallback mechanism provides the Commission with the ability to fill gaps if the standardisation process does not deliver in time.

## Implications for Organisations

1. **Do not wait for harmonised standards** — whether the binding date is 2 August 2026 (pre-Omnibus) or 2 December 2027 (Omnibus), it will not be postponed further if standards are delayed.
2. **Build on ISO 42001 and existing frameworks** — use international standards as a foundation for your compliance programme, knowing that harmonised standards will build upon similar principles.
3. **Document everything** — in the absence of harmonised standards, your ability to demonstrate compliance depends on thorough documentation of your risk management, data governance, testing, and monitoring processes.
4. **Participate in standardisation** — if your organisation has the resources, participation in JTC 21 through your national standardisation body provides early access to draft standards and influence over their content.
5. **Plan for adoption** — build your compliance programme with the flexibility to adopt harmonised standards as they are published, without having to rebuild from scratch.
