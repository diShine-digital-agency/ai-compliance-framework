# EU AI Act Roles: Provider, Deployer, Importer, Distributor

The EU AI Act assigns different obligations to different actors in the AI value chain. Understanding which role applies to your organisation is essential for determining your compliance obligations.

## Role Definitions

### Provider (Article 3(3))

A **provider** is any natural or legal person, public authority, agency, or other body that **develops an AI system or a general-purpose AI model** (or has one developed on its behalf) and **places it on the market** or **puts it into service** under its own name or trademark, whether for payment or free of charge.

**Key obligations:**
- Ensure the system complies with the Act's requirements before placing it on the market
- Conduct conformity assessment (for high-risk systems)
- Prepare technical documentation (Annex IV)
- Implement a quality management system (Article 17)
- Register the system in the EU AI Database (Article 49)
- Implement post-market monitoring (Article 72)
- Report serious incidents (Article 73)
- Affix CE marking (Article 48)
- Draw up the EU Declaration of Conformity (Article 47)

### Deployer (Article 3(4))

A **deployer** is any natural or legal person, public authority, agency, or other body **using an AI system under its authority**, except where the system is used in the course of a personal, non-professional activity.

**Key obligations:**
- Use the system in accordance with the instructions of use provided by the provider
- Assign human oversight to natural persons with the necessary competence, training, and authority (Article 26(2))
- Ensure input data is relevant and sufficiently representative (Article 26(4))
- Monitor the operation of the system and report malfunctions or serious incidents to the provider and the national authority (Article 26(5))
- Conduct a Fundamental Rights Impact Assessment before deployment in certain cases (Article 27)
- Keep logs generated by the system for at least six months (Article 26(6))
- Inform natural persons that they are subject to a high-risk AI system (Article 26(7))
- Conduct a Data Protection Impact Assessment when required by GDPR (Article 26(9))

### Importer (Article 3(6))

An **importer** is any natural or legal person located or established in the EU that places on the market an AI system bearing the name or trademark of a natural or legal person established **outside the EU**.

**Key obligations:**
- Verify that the provider has carried out the conformity assessment, drawn up the technical documentation, and affixed the CE marking
- Verify that the provider has designated an authorised representative in the EU
- Not place a system on the market unless satisfied it complies with the Act
- Inform the provider and the market surveillance authority when a system presents a risk

### Distributor (Article 3(7))

A **distributor** is any natural or legal person in the supply chain, other than the provider or the importer, that **makes an AI system available on the EU market**.

**Key obligations:**
- Verify that the CE marking has been affixed
- Verify that the required documentation accompanies the system
- Not make a system available if it has reason to believe it does not comply
- Inform the provider or importer and the market surveillance authority when a system presents a risk

### Authorised Representative (Article 3(5))

An **authorised representative** is any natural or legal person located or established in the EU who has received and accepted a written mandate from a provider established outside the EU to act on the provider's behalf for the purposes of the Act.

## When a Deployer Becomes a Provider

An important subtlety: a deployer can become a provider (and inherit provider obligations) in certain situations:

1. **The deployer places its own name or trademark** on a high-risk AI system already placed on the market.
2. **The deployer makes a substantial modification** to a high-risk AI system.
3. **The deployer modifies the intended purpose** of an AI system in a way that makes it high-risk.

This means that organisations that fine-tune, customise, or rebrand AI systems may be treated as providers under the Act.

## Special Case: GPAI Providers

Providers of General-Purpose AI models have a separate set of obligations under Articles 51–55. These are distinct from the high-risk system obligations and focus on transparency, copyright compliance, and (for systemic risk models) safety and security measures. See [General Purpose AI (GPAI)](General-Purpose-AI-GPAI).

## Special Case: SaaS and Cloud Providers

The Technology sector addendum in the repository explores the Provider vs. Deployer distinction for SaaS and cloud companies in detail. A SaaS company offering an AI-powered HR screening tool is a **provider** of a high-risk system. A company that purchases and uses that tool is a **deployer**. See [Sector Guide: Technology](Sector-Guide-Technology).
