# National Implementation by Member States

The EU AI Act requires each Member State to designate national competent authorities responsible for enforcement. By **2 August 2025**, every Member State was required to designate at least one **market surveillance authority** and one **notifying authority**.

## Required National Authorities

| Authority Type | Role |
|---|---|
| **Market Surveillance Authority** | Oversee compliance with the AI Act for AI systems; monitor for prohibited practices; supervise high-risk AI systems; investigate complaints; impose penalties |
| **Notifying Authority** | Designate and supervise conformity assessment bodies (Notified Bodies) that certify AI systems before market entry |
| **Fundamental Rights Authority** | Enforce AI-related fundamental rights obligations for high-risk systems |

## Designation Status

Implementation progress varies significantly across Member States. As of early 2026:

### Early Movers

- **Italy:** Among the first to designate national authorities and adopt implementing legislation. The Agenzia per l'Italia Digitale (AgID) and the Autorità Garante per la Protezione dei Dati Personali (GPDP) play central roles.
- **Denmark:** Early designator of national authorities with a clear governance structure.
- **Spain:** Created a dedicated new public body — the Agencia Española de Supervisión de Inteligencia Artificial (AESIA) — specifically for AI oversight, established in 2023 ahead of the Act's entry into force.

### Decentralised Models

- **Germany:** Designated the Federal Network Agency (Bundesnetzagentur, BNetzA) as the primary market surveillance authority for AI, with domain-specific authorities retaining oversight for their fields (e.g., BaFin for financial services AI, BAuA for workplace AI).
- **France:** Multiple authorities involved, including CNIL (data protection), ARCEP (digital regulation), and sector-specific regulators.
- **Netherlands:** The Authority for Consumers and Markets (ACM) takes a lead role, coordinating with sector-specific regulators.

### Later Stage

- **Belgium, Austria, Portugal, and others:** At various stages of consultation and legislative drafting as of late 2025 / early 2026.
- Most Member States were still finalising their implementing legislation as of the first quarter of 2026.

### Other Notable Member States

- **Ireland:** The Department of Enterprise, Trade and Employment leads on AI Act implementation. Given Ireland's role as the European headquarters for many US technology companies (Google, Meta, Microsoft, Apple), the Irish AI regulatory approach will be particularly significant for the technology sector. The Data Protection Commission (DPC) will coordinate on GDPR–AI Act overlap.
- **Luxembourg:** The Institut Luxembourgeois de Régulation (ILR) is involved in AI oversight, relevant for the country's financial services and fund management sector.
- **Sweden:** The Swedish Post and Telecom Authority (PTS) is among the authorities designated for AI oversight. Sweden has been an early adopter of AI in public services, creating practical experience with AI governance.
- **Finland:** Has taken a proactive approach with the Ministry of Economic Affairs and Employment coordinating AI policy. Finland's AuroraAI programme for government AI services provides practical deployment experience.
- **Poland:** The Office of Electronic Communications (UKE) is expected to play a role in AI oversight. As the EU's fifth-largest economy and a growing technology hub, Poland's implementation approach will have significant practical impact.

## EU-Level Governance

| Body | Function |
|---|---|
| **European AI Office** | Part of the European Commission; oversees GPAI model enforcement, coordinates cross-border cases, issues guidelines, manages the EU AI Database |
| **European Artificial Intelligence Board** | Composed of representatives from each Member State; ensures harmonised application across the EU; advises the Commission |
| **Scientific Panel of Independent Experts** | Advises the AI Office and national authorities on GPAI models, particularly regarding systemic risk classification and model evaluation |
| **Advisory Forum** | Provides stakeholder input from industry, civil society, and academia |

## Regulatory Sandboxes (Article 57)

Member States are required to establish at least one **AI regulatory sandbox** by 2 August 2026. Regulatory sandboxes provide controlled environments where innovative AI systems can be tested under regulatory supervision before full market deployment.

Key features:
- Supervised by national competent authorities
- Time-limited and scope-limited
- Data processing in sandboxes must still comply with GDPR
- Available to both startups and established companies
- Designed to facilitate innovation while ensuring safety

Spain, the Netherlands, and France were among the first to announce sandbox programmes.

## Implications for Organisations

1. **Track designation status** in every Member State where your AI systems are deployed. The competent authority (and its enforcement priorities and culture) may differ significantly between countries.
2. **Expect varying enforcement intensity** in the initial years. Some Member States will enforce more actively than others, but the EU AI Office provides a harmonisation mechanism.
3. **National implementing legislation** may add requirements beyond the AI Act's baseline (the Act sets minimum standards; Member States can go further).
4. **Multiple authorities may have jurisdiction** over the same AI system (e.g., the market surveillance authority and the data protection authority), requiring coordination.
