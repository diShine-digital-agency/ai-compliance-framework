# AI Literacy Obligation (Article 4)

Article 4 of the EU AI Act establishes a **horizontal AI literacy obligation** that applies to all providers and deployers of AI systems, regardless of the system's risk classification. This obligation has been in force since **2 February 2025**.

## What Article 4 Requires

> *"Providers and deployers of AI systems shall take measures to ensure, to the best extent possible, a sufficient level of AI literacy of their staff and other persons dealing with the operation and use of AI systems on their behalf, taking into account their technical knowledge, experience, education and training and the context the AI systems are to be used in, and considering the persons or groups of persons on whom the AI systems are to be used."*

## Key Elements

### Who Must Comply
- **All providers** of AI systems (developers, manufacturers)
- **All deployers** of AI systems (organisations using AI)
- Regardless of risk classification — this obligation applies to minimal, limited, and high-risk systems equally

### Who Must Be Trained
- Staff **dealing with the operation** of AI systems
- Staff **dealing with the use** of AI systems
- Other persons acting **on behalf** of the provider or deployer

### What "Sufficient Level" Means
The required level of AI literacy is context-dependent. It must account for:
- The person's **technical knowledge, experience, education, and training**
- The **context** in which the AI systems are used
- The **persons or groups** on whom the AI systems are to be used

A data scientist working on model development requires a different level of literacy than a customer service agent using an AI chatbot. Both need training, but the content and depth differ.

## Practical Implementation

### Role-Based Training Approach

The repository's [AI Literacy Programme template](https://github.com/diShine-digital-agency/ai-compliance-framework/blob/main/templates/ai-literacy-programme.md) defines six stakeholder groups with differentiated training needs:

| Role Group | Training Focus |
|---|---|
| **Executive / Board** | Governance, liability exposure, strategic implications, risk oversight |
| **Legal / Compliance** | Regulatory obligations, enforcement timeline, contractual requirements |
| **Technical (Engineering, Data Science)** | Model governance, bias audit, technical documentation, adversarial robustness |
| **Procurement** | Vendor assessment, DPA requirements, GPAI obligations |
| **Operational (HR, Marketing, Customer Service)** | Responsible use, data classification, prohibited practices, human oversight |
| **All Staff** | Basic AI concepts, acceptable use policy, IP protection, how to report incidents |

### Four-Module Core Curriculum

| Module | Content |
|---|---|
| **Module 1: AI Fundamentals** | What AI is, how it works (at a level appropriate to the audience), capabilities and limitations, common misconceptions |
| **Module 2: Regulatory Landscape** | EU AI Act overview, risk classification, prohibited practices, enforcement timeline, GDPR interaction, penalty structure |
| **Module 3: Responsible AI for Technical Teams** | Data governance, bias detection and mitigation, model documentation, privacy-preserving techniques, adversarial robustness |
| **Module 4: Human Oversight in Practice** | What human oversight means operationally, when to override AI decisions, how to interpret AI outputs, escalation procedures |

### Assessment and Evidence

To demonstrate compliance, organisations should:
1. **Needs assessment** — document the AI literacy requirements for each role
2. **Training delivery** — record attendance, completion, and assessment scores
3. **Training register** — maintain a register of all AI literacy activities
4. **Periodic reassessment** — update training as regulations, technologies, and organisational AI usage evolve
5. **Documentation** — retain evidence of compliance for potential regulatory inspection

### Continuous Updates

AI literacy is not a one-time event. Training must be updated when:
- New AI systems are deployed
- Regulations change (new Commission guidelines, standards)
- New risks are identified (incidents, bias discoveries)
- The organisation's AI usage expands or changes

## Enforcement

While the AI literacy obligation does not have a specific penalty tier, failure to ensure adequate AI literacy could:
- Contribute to non-compliance with other obligations (e.g., inadequate human oversight for high-risk systems results from insufficient literacy)
- Be considered an aggravating factor in enforcement actions
- Undermine the organisation's defence in liability claims
